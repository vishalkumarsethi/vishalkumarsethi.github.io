What I'm reading: 

Efficacy and safety of adjunctive ezogabine (retigabine) in refractory partial epilepsy. Brodie et. al.  Neurology doi.org/10.1212/WNL.0b013e3181fd6170
Cool.  I have always been a fan of companies working to broaden the # of indications a drug treats, hence what XENE is doing. For an example, look at the space of autoimmune disorders. There is a LOT of overlap of Humira/Enbrel/Remicade/Entyvio/Xeljanz/etc. in treating AI disorders, but then the question of whether a drug should treat a specific indication VERY WELL and whether it will somewhat work/not work at all for other indications OR the idea that drugs can equally treat multiple conditions. Usually it seems to be the former (obviously it depends on the conditions and the specifics) but there is definitely overlap. I'll talk about it later a bit but LOXO (a very successful startup over the past 5 years~) worked to treat a type of tumor based on its genes and not necessarily a type of cancer. 

Anything related to KCNQ2 conditions (classifies essentially neonatal epilepsy and neonatal epileptic encephalopathy. 
https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK32534/
Very cool.  I am in huge support and ABSOLUTELY LOVE research/R&D work on pediatric orphan conditions. 

As you can may be able to tell, a drug company broadening a pre-approved drug to pediatric orphan diseases? Actually heroes. Period. Thank god we have huge financial incentives for healthcare; otherwise no one would care about sick people, ESPECIALLY groups of small but VERY sick people. Things always fall back to the main dogma:

PRICE * VOLUME = SALES

If this didn't hold true in healthcare then everyone would just treat high cholesterol and obesity. But, thanks to innovation and progress in the past few decades, we have drugs like Folotyn and Naglazyme and Cerezyme and we need Cerezyme to cost $334,000 (wikipedia quotes $200K annual cost so take #s with a grain of salt. It's expensive to say the least) for lifetime (~20 yrs hence around $6-7million total lifetime cost (?)) since only .0025% of the population gets it. Yeah. .0025%. 1 in 40K live births. So if your kid ends up getting type 1 Gaucher disease, you will do/pay anyone anything to help your child. 
BTW, Gaucher disease is a genetic disorder requiring enzyme replacement therapy~ (think clotting factor replacement therapy for hemophilia patients). 

The reason I bring this up is that people sometimes don't understand drug pricing and are just very misunderstood. See this chart below. Don't concern yourself with the details. Disclaimer: I am not an expert on this but I am pretty sure I am not a complete novice either. 



The takeaway is a drug company needs a VERY VERY LARGE initial investment in drug development, then when approved it will make money first to recoup the money spent, THEN it will generate profit for x years until patent expiry. 

There are MANY exceptions and details which make this chart a very simplified/general concept, but the idea of how drug companies can make money AND make drugs still holds. 

On the same vein, the whole concept of insurance (I really like how this is said) is to account for exotic risk. Everyone pays their monthly premiums because no one wants to be the patient who has to pay cash for their PNH treatment ($500,000 per year for life). The reason Soliris costs a bunch of money is because for the ~500 people diagnosed per year with PNH, if no treatment was available they would die. No god will save or cure them. And there is no motivation for Alexion (ALXN) to treat such a small condition (read: LOW VOLUME) without charging a high $ (read: PRICE) in order to maximize...? Sales of course. 

Hope this is informative and clears up some misconceptions. There is much much MUCH more to talk about here and the system is not without MANY MANY faults. But one does what they can I suppose.
